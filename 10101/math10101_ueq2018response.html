MATH10101 2018/19: Lecturers' response to the unit evaluation questionnaires

1. Our message to the 2018/19 students

This course continues to be taught by two lecturers. Prof. Louise Walker taught the 1st half of the course, and Dr Yuri Bazlov taught the second half. While the content remained largely unchanged compared to last year, one important new feature of the course were short feedback quizzes in lectures. Students were given the opportunity to do a few Kahoot quizzes after the break between two consecutive lecture hours. See comments about the quizzes below.

We were glad to see that the students were very eager to ask questions. Many students asked questions in lectures, after lectures, by email, or, towards the end of the semester, in the Online Forum - and some answered other students' questions in the Forum! This helped to make sure that the course was an intellectual process of learning, not (just) of memorising the material.

2. The questionnaire

You were asked to indicate how strongly you agree with statements about the course, on a scale from 5 (agree), 4 (mostly agree) to 1 (disagree). We thank everyone who returned their feedback: more than half of students did so, making the feedback scores (see below) a reliable indicator of how the course went. The responses were overwhelmingly positive, with a lot of praise in the textual comments. Here we will mostly focus on suggestions for improvement.

Overall, I would rate this unit as being excellent: 4.4 out of 5 (agree: 51%, agree or mostly agree: 92%).

Issued raised in the questionnaires:

  • Too many theorems/proofs, difficult to learn and practise them all.

    This is an issue which needs to be addressed in two courses, MATH10101 and MATH10111, simultaneously. A number of students felt that too much was packed into the course, especially towards the end, and there were requests to make more time/opportunities available to practise proofs. This will be under review.

  • More examples in lectures please.

    This course is supported by supervisions which are designed so that the students can work through examples. That said, there is never too many examples in lectures. It may be a good idea to more often highlight links between the lecture material and the supervision problem sheets. Students were generally happy with Kahoot quizzes as an extra source of examples, but...

  • Yuri's Kahoot quizzes were two difficult.

    A couple of such comments were seen, while no one said they were too easy! No problem - will review the feedback quizzes to make sure they can be answered effectively within 30-60 seconds.

The feedback that I received on my work was helpful: 4.3 out of 5 (agree: 53%, a/ma: 89%)

The support classes were helpful: 4.4 out of 5 (agree: 61%, a/ma: 86%)

Obviously the students' experience varied between supervision groups, some praising the style of their supervisor while some others finding it difficult to adjust to. Weekly supervisions are the main vehicle for feedback in this course, and there is a lot of value in getting supervision feedback from someone else than the lecturer. Nevertheless, despite many positive comments, there is room for improvement in terms of feedback, and we will continue to improve supervision problem sheets and work with group supervisors to make sure that students benefit even more from supervisions.

The unit was well organised: 4.6 out of 5 (agree: 67%, a/ma: 95%)

Glad to see enthusiastic feedback here - running a big course like MATH10101 is a huge logistical effort.

The course materials were helpful: 4.6 out of 5 (agree: 65%, a/ma: 94%)

In comments, students praised the quality of typed lecture notes and supervision problem sheets. Some said that the problem sheets were too easy, and some that they were too hard, but overall it seems that the level of challenge was right.

The eLearning resources provided in this unit were helpful: 4.2 out of 5 (agree: 47%, a/ma: 78%)

This is the lowest score in the feedback. The students were generally unhappy with the platform via which the course text, Introduction to Mathematical Reasoning, was provided.
