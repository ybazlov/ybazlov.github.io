Comments on MATH10101 Foundations of Pure Mathematics A, Weeks 7-12, Semester 1 2017/18

Mon 22 January 2018

by Yuri Bazlov

1. My message to the 2017/18 students

This was the second year in which MATH10101 ran in a refreshed format. Prof. Nige Ray taught the 1st half of the course; my feedback relates to the 2nd half which I taught. This year, I made slight changes to the content in order to better harmonise it with the material on Cryptography the students were working on in MATH10001 Mathematical Workshop.

I was truly impressed by your enthusiasm and your motivation. Quite a number of students were active in the classroom, asking questions and making comments. Even more students got in touch with me by email from time to time. This definitely helped me to gauge the level of understanding and to adjust the pace. Here is some advice you may find useful in the rest of your degree course:

  1. Don’t be shy, ask even more questions and make even more comments to your lecturers. Remember, there is no such thing as a stupid question.
  2. Start revising the material early: if the lectures finish in mid-December, don’t wait until January. Make more use of the Forum on Blackboard to ask revision questions. Early questions asked in the Forum allowed me to give detailed responses with extra examples; this is easier, and more useful, than a quick answer a day or two before the exam.

In the questionnaire, you were asked to indicate how strongly you agree with statements about the course, on a scale from 5 (agree), 4 (mostly agree) to 1 (disagree). I thank everyone who returned their feedback: two thirds of students did so, making the feedback scores (see below) a reliable indicator of how the course went. The responses were overwhelmingly positive, with a lot of praise in the textual comments; here I will only focus on 'What can be improved?’ comments specifically relevant to the second half of the course.

Overall, I would rate this unit as being excellent: 4.4 out of 5 (agree: 53%, agree or mostly agree: 89%). Issued raised in the questionnaires:

  • Too much content, hard to learn it all. I’d say I could further streamline the content of the second half of the course, but not reduce the amount. Some of the material is needed for the Mathematical Workshop, some required in subsequent courses, all is necessary for a thorough introduction to mathematics and understanding modern applications. Remember, this course expects you to invest 145 hours into independent study during weeks 1-12. Also, you should use the revision period more efficiently, see 1b above.
  • Exam practice could be introduced earlier on in the course. In fact, I like to use past exam questions for practice (not all lecturers do). This year, only one Foundations of Pure Mathematics A past exam paper was available. The more past papers are available, the more past exam questions can be used in the course.
  • More examples/exercises on abstract concepts. More links between the content covered in lectures and the content appearing on problem sheets. Good point. I’ll address it by highlighting the links between the lecture material and the supervision problem sheets.
  • A recap on some of the earlier material at the end would be useful. Nice idea but difficult to implement given the setup with two lecturers.
  • Do not give long proofs. Proofs are essential for understanding mathematics. Some proofs are long, although I am constantly improving the structure of the course, including proofs.
  • Introduce more group theory. I do not think that this is a good idea – there are other beautiful things to learn in Foundations. Wait until MATH20201 Algebraic Structures 1.

The feedback that I received on my work was helpful: 4.3 out of 5 (agree: 49%, a/ma: 84%)

The support classes were helpful: 4.4 out of 5 (agree: 60%, a/ma: 88%)

For the 2nd half of the course, the weekly supervisions are the main vehicle for feedback. Feedback was the only category where less than 50% chose to "agree". We will need to improve this further.

  • My supervisor was helpful/not helpful. I cannot deal with these comments anonymously – please get in touch with me, confidentially, to discuss.

The unit was well organised: 4.5 out of 5 (agree: 63%, a/ma: 93%)

Glad to see enthusiastic feedback here. There is always an issue of two 2-hour lectures vs. four 1-hour lectures (or a different split). Overall, I feel that there is a slight balance in favour of the current setup of 2-hour blocks (with breaks in the middle of each hour) as it makes the unit more organised and streamlined.

The course materials were helpful: 4.5 out of 5 (agree: 60%, a/ma: 95%)

  • Follow the PJE book more closely/Introduce more variation from the PJE book. Those comments 'cancel each other’. Overall, I think that the course achieved a good balance.
  • Draft notes could be uploaded earlier. Will do, but access to draft notes before the lectures is a luxury which I happen to provide. Copies of what I wrote in the lecture, as well as podcasts, were available shortly after each lecture.
  • Set a long revision homework after the last week. Interesting idea. Past exam papers may also be used, although I don't mind giving students as much work as they want.

The eLearning resources provided in this unit were helpful...

Will not comment on this as virtually no eLearning resources were provided. Online PDF notes and podcasts are not eLearning as they lack interactivity. The only eLearning resource was the Forum on Blackboard but it became really active only after the questionnaires closed. I think that it would be useful to offer students lots of practice questions via the Maple TA online software, and will work on that.

Yuri Bazlov’s teaching was excellent: 4.6 out of 5 (agree: 64%, a/ma: 92%)

  • Bazlov’s lectures are hard to follow. While I am pleased with the feedback (my teaching was rated higher than any other aspect of the course), I am constantly working to make my teaching even better.
  • Pace can be slowed down. An isolated comment. Although there were no suggestions that the pace was too slow, I think that overall, given the availability of podcasts and of everything I wrote in a lecture as PDF, my pace is about right.
  • Yuri, take teaching classes from Nigel. I did! Nige led one of the classes for new lecturers when I came to Manchester. Nige's teaching of MATH10101 will no doubt be sorely missed – we all wish him happy retirement.
